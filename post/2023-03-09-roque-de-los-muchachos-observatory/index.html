A Weekend Working at Roque de los Muchachos Observatory

By Pedro Mas Buitrago

March 9, 2023

This January, I had the great opportunity to work for two nights at the Nordic Optical Telescope (NOT), one of the many telescopes located at Roque de los Muchachos Observatory on La Palma (Canary Islands). Apart from being one of the most renowned observatories in the world, Roque stands out for it unique location. At an altitude of 2396 meters above sea level, the landscape of the Taburiente National Park and the sea of clouds characteristic of La Palma, which is formed due to the exceptional conditions of the island, are truly breathtaking.

Day 1

Since I was a little short on time, the first thing I did when I landed in La Palma was to take the rental car, have something to eat and start the long way up to the observatory. After a few fallen rocks and many, many curves, I arrived at the observatory residence, dropped my stuff and headed to the telescope, which was a 10-minute drive away. Here I met Jerry, a pre-doctoral student at Instituto de Astrofísica de Canarias (IAC), who was to be my partner in work and adventure for the next two days. David, the support astronomer, was waiting for us at the telescope and showed us the area and the support building, where you can prepare yourself a coffee or something to eat during the night.

We then proceeded to prepare the observations for the night and take the necessary calibration frames. Our goal was to obtain low-resolution spectra for eight target stars, so a crucial step in these observing campaigns is to plan the total exposure time, the number of exposures and the optimal observing hours for each object. This done, we took the car and headed back to the residence for dinner.

Daily schedules in these observing campaigns are upside down. You work all night and sleep during the day. In the afternoon, you take calibration frames, if necessary, have dinner and grab a bag of food for the night.

The night of observation began and so did my nerves, since it was my first experience with such a telescope and I wanted to obtain data of the highest possible quality. Here, the assistance of the support astronomers was key, as they stayed with us during the beginning of the first night and gave us a lot of advice that ended up being quite useful.

The willingness to help in an altruistic way is not something easy to find, and its abundance in the scientific world is a differential factor compared to other professional sectors.

Fortunately, the weather conditions were ideal during the night (also the second one) and we had a very successful night. We even got some images of the comet C/2022 E3 (ZTF) using the ALFOSC camera.

8:30 a.m., time to go to bed.

Day 2

3:30 p.m., time to get up.

Jerry and I had a quick breakfast(?) and enjoyed some time strolling around Roque de los Muchachos to admire its natural surroundings. Calibrations, dinner, and here we are back in the NOT control room. Operate a telescope is not trivial, and can vary a lot from telescope to telescope. In the case of NOT, you can pre-prepare observations in scripts, so switching between different objects or configurations on site is very automatic.

Since each exposure can take several hours, the nights of observations can be very long, so you need to have a back-up-do-not-fall-asleep plan in your back pocket. As always, music was the answer. Shinova, Sexy Zebras and Belle and Sebastian came to the rescue, providing the soundtrack for the night.

Another excellent night of observation. Our work was completed and our goals were accomplished, so at 7:30 a.m. we closed the telescope and returned to the residence, but not before beholding one of the most beautiful sunrises I have ever seen.

Yes, those are the Gran Telescopio de Canarias (GTC) and the sun rising next to the Teide volcano.

Day 3

With the observations completed, I spent most of the day analyzing and sharing with my team some of the quick results obtained (to get the final results, you must properly reduce your spectra, which can take several days). In the afternoon, we were very lucky and were able to take a tour inside the GTC, which is the largest optical and infrared telescope in the world to date. Here, we got to meet other colleagues from the IAC and ended up accompanying one of them on her night of observation at the Telescopio Nazionale Galileo (TNG).

My last day at Roque de los Muchachos had come to an end.

Day 4

My return flight was early the next day, so I decided to stay the last night in a small hotel in Santa Cruz de La Palma, which is a 15-minute drive from the airport, and spend this last day exploring the southwestern region of the island. The natural landscape of this area was severely affected by the Cumbre Vieja volcanic eruption, that took place between September and December 2021, and even a year later it is really shocking to go there.

9:00 a.m., I took my inseparable Opel and start my way down, this time on the opposite road to the one I took on the first day. Once down, I stopped in a small village to ask the locals for the best spots to see the volcano. All of them agreed that the best option is to first have a coffee at the “Mirador del Time”, from where you can admire from afar the Cumbre Vieja volcanic ridge, and then explore some small roads from where you can have a closer view of it. After that well-deserved coffee, very tasty by the way (The manager told me that “it was the best coffee I was going to drink in my life”), I set course to the affected area, and I was surprised by the provisional roads that have been built over the solidified lava flows. From one of these roads I had my first close-up view of the volcano.

The landscape was desolate, full of black ashes and huge lava rocks, but the contrast between this black and the intense blue of the sea endowed the environment with an intrinsic beauty that kept you spellbound for minutes.

Finally, after exploring the area a bit, I found a small trail near a road and walked along it for a while. This is where I got the most impressive view, with the volcano rising in front of me (as my nephew says: “High! High!”), still spewing vapors from its numerous vents. You could even see the tracks that the more massive rocks had left on the ashes on their way downhill.

I stayed here until the first drop of rain reminded me of something: Real Madrid was playing in an hour and I still had to head to Santa Cruz de La Palma and find somewhere to watch the game, so I retraced my steps and set off to the last destination of this this short yet inspiring trip.
